SOC 310 - Introduction to Social Work

Course Outline

Definition: Social work is a profession dedicated to helping individuals, families, and communities improve their well-being and social functioning.

Scope: It covers healthcare, education, community development, and social justice initiatives.

Real-life Example: A social worker helping a family find affordable housing or assisting children in foster care.

Origin & Evolution: Social work began in the 19th century to address poverty and social inequality.

Western Growth: Developed professional standards and organizations in Europe & USA.

Africa & Nigeria: Introduced through colonial influence; now includes government and NGO interventions.

Example: Establishment of NGOs in Nigeria to support child welfare and education programs.

Core Values: Respect for human dignity, social justice, integrity, and compassion.

Ethical Principles: Ensure confidentiality, fairness, and advocacy for vulnerable populations.

Example: Supporting victims of domestic violence while protecting their privacy.

Social Work in Various Fields: Social workers operate in healthcare, education, industry, military, corrections, and community programs.

Social Welfare Agencies: Government and non-governmental organizations provide support services such as counseling, housing, and food aid.

Roles of Social Workers: They help individuals and groups solve problems, advocate for rights, and connect people with resources.

Example: A social worker in a school helping students with emotional challenges or linking families to community health services.

Casework (Individual): Working one-on-one with clients to solve personal or family issues.

Group Work: Helping groups of people with similar problems, like support groups for addiction recovery.

Community Organization: Planning and leading community projects to improve living conditions.

Social Welfare Administration: Managing programs and services that help vulnerable populations.

Social Work Research: Studying social problems to create effective solutions.

Example: Running a community literacy program or a youth mentorship group.

Steps in Helping: Engagement, assessment, planning, intervention, evaluation, and termination.

Skills Required: Listening carefully, showing empathy, interviewing, clear communication, and problem-solving.

Example: A social worker helping a family plan a budget, connecting them to resources, following up, and gradually ending direct support once the family is self-sufficient.

Social Problems: Poverty, unemployment, family breakdown, child abuse, gender-based violence, drug abuse, and crime.

Social Work Responses: Social workers provide support, advocacy, counseling, and connect people to resources to address these issues.

Example: Helping families access job training programs, counseling abused children, or organizing community crime-prevention initiatives.

Globalization: Social workers face challenges like migration, cultural diversity, and global social issues.

Human Rights & Advocacy: Promoting equality, protecting vulnerable populations, and speaking against injustice.

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs): Social workers support education, health, gender equality, and poverty reduction initiatives.

Challenges: Limited resources, lack of professional recognition, and social inequality.

Professionalization & Regulation: Efforts to establish standards, licensing, and professional associations for social workers in Nigeria.

Opportunities: Growth in specialized areas such as child protection, mental health, community development, and policy advocacy.

Example: Young social workers creating NGOs to address youth unemployment or mental health awareness programs in Nigerian communities.
